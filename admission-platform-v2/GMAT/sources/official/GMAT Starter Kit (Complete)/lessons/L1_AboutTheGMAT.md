# An Overview of the Basics

These frequently asked questions will help you understand what the GMAT exam is all about.

*Why take the GMAT exam?*
1. The GMAT exam helps you stand out.  It helps you demonstrate your readiness, commitment, motivation, and ability to succeed in business school.  
2. It shows admissions officers that you’re truly motivated to pursue graduate management education. The GMAT exam is specifically designed to assess your academic potential for advanced study in business and management. GMAT scores are used by more than 7,400 graduate management programs at approximately 2,400 schools. 
3. It helps you refresh your study skills, especially if you’ve been out of the university for some time. It will help you prepare for the rigors of business school.
 
*What is the test designed to measure about me?*
The GMAT exam measures higher-order reasoning skills that faculty, admissions professionals, and employers worldwide have identified as important for incoming students. You’ll need basic quantitative and verbal skills, but the difficulty of the GMAT exam comes from the required analytical reasoning abilities, which are developed over time.

# How is the Test Structured?
The exam has three sections, which tests Verbal Reasoning, Quantitative Reasoning, and Data Insights. These sections test skills including critical thinking, data analysis, and problem-solving, which all call for complex judgement. It takes about two hours, fifteen minutes. All three GMAT sections are computer adaptive which means they customize themselves to your ability level. 

Immediately prior to starting your exam, you will be given the option to choose the order in which you will take your GMAT exam.

# What is the Scoring Scale?
When you take the GMAT you will get four different scores: 

- Individual section scores for Data Insights, Verbal Reasoning, and Quantitative Reasoning
- Each section is scored on a scale between 60 to 90, in 1-point increments
- Your GMAT Total score is based on your three section scores, and ranges between 205 and 805, in 10-point increments. It is not a simple sum of the Data Insights, Verbal, and Quantitative scores.

*TIP*
Remember that your GMAT scores are just one tool schools use to try to predict how well you might perform in their programs. Admissions officers consider your scores plus your undergraduate record, application essays, interviews, and letters of recommendation. To find out more about actual application reviews, contact the admissions office at each school to which you’re applying.

# More About the GMAT
*Is the GMAT exam available in different languages?*
No. The exam is conducted entirely in English, all instructions are in English, and you must enter all your answers using standard English characters. 

*What doesn’t the GMAT exam do?* 
The test does not:
- Assume that you have any specific knowledge of business or other specialized content areas 
- Measure your achievement in any particular subject area (schools look at your university transcript for that) 
- Measure subjective factors that will be important to your academic and career success—such as motivation, creativity, interpersonal skills, study skills, or overall success on the job  

# Who is Behind the GMAT Exam?
It’s owned by the Graduate Management Admission Council (GMAC), a global nonprofit organization composed of graduate business schools around the world. 

Founded in 1953, GMAC is actively committed to advancing the art and science of admissions by convening and representing the industry and offering best-in-class products and services for schools and students.

The GMAT(TM) Exam is relied on by more than 7,700 graduate programs worldwide, and 9 out of 10 new MBA enrollments globally are made using a GMAT score.* The GMAT is the most widely used exam for graduate business school admissions, and more than 200,000 candidates take the GMAT exam every year.

*TIP*
Don’t do anything that might threaten your future in a graduate management program. This software and other study materials released by GMAC contain questions that have been retired from the GMAT exam. Accessing live Data Insights, Quantitative, or Verbal test questions in advance—or sharing test content during or after you take the test—is a serious violation, which could cause your scores to be canceled, schools to be notified, and you to be banned from further testing.

# Understanding the GMAT Structure and Computer Adaptive Format
To do your best, familiarize yourself with aspects of the GMAT exam that may make it different from other tests you’ve taken.

## Test Structure
The GMAT exam is organized into three timed sections to measure your analytical reasoning ability and quantitative and verbal skills. Counting one optional scheduled break, you should plan to spend about 145 minutes taking the exam.

*The GMAT Sections at a Glance*
| GMAT Section       | Number of Questions | Time Allotted  | What the Section Measures                     |
|--------------------|---------------------|----------------|-----------------------------------------------|
| (Data Sufficiency, Multi-Source Reasoning, Table Analysis, Graphics Interpretation, and Two-Part Analysis)       | 20                  | 45 minutes     | Your ability to assess multiple sources and types of information—graphic, numeric, and verbal—as they relate to one another, to ultimately make informed decisions       |
| Quantitative Reasoning | 21                  | 45 minutes     | Your algebraic and arithmetic foundational knowledge and how you apply this knowledge to solve problems       |
| Verbal Reasoning       | 23                  | 45 minutes     | Your ability to read and comprehend written material, analyze and evaluate complex scenarios, and draw logical inferences arguments, or conclusions       |
| Total Exam Time 	|  64 questions	|	2 hours, 15 minutes	| |

Immediately prior to starting your exam, you will be given the option to choose which order you will take your GMAT exam.

*TIP*
Keep in mind these rules about exam break. You may take only one break during the exam. If you don't come back within the time limit, the time will be deducted from the next section of your exam. But if you decide not to take a break, you won't receive additional time to work on the exam.

You can now bookmark any questions you feel unsure about during the exam. You can review your answers at the end of each section. You can review and edit answers even to questions you have not bookmarked, but bookmarking a question helps you find it again quickly. You can bookmark and review as many questions as you like, but you can only change your answers to three questions per section. You must finish all your bookmarking, reviewing, and editing within each section’s time limit. No extra time is given to use these features.

## Computer Adaptive Format
The GMAT exam is computer adaptive, which has three important implications for you as a test taker: 

1.  *The GMAT exam constantly and automatically adapts questions to your individual ability level.*
    The Verbal, Data Insights, and Quantitative sections are each item-level adaptive. These sections draw from a large bank of questions and administer items that are tailored to your ability level, which means you won’t get many questions that are much too hard or too easy for you.

    Here’s how it works: The first question you receive will be of medium difficulty. As you answer each question, the computer scores your answer and uses it—as well as your responses to any preceding questions—to select the next question. If you answer the first question correctly, the computer will usually give you a slightly harder question. If you answer the first question incorrectly, your next question will be slightly easier. This process continues until you complete the section, at which point the computer will have an accurate assessment of your ability in that subject.

2.  *The computer adaptive sections will become more difficult as you answer more questions correctly.*
    But if you get a question that seems easier, it doesn’t necessarily mean you answered the last question incorrectly. The test has to cover a range of content, both the type of question asked and the subject matter presented. Your score is determined by the difficulty and statistical characteristics of the questions you answer as well as the number of questions you answer correctly.

3.  *You may not skip, return to, or change your responses to previous questions until you finish the section.*
    This is because the computer uses your response to each question to select the next one. Once you have completed a section, you may go back and review any question(s) you like, and you will be permitted to change your answer to up to three questions per section.

*TIP*
What happens on a computer adaptive exam if you either make a mistake or take a guess? Whether you answer a question incorrectly by mistake or correctly by a lucky guess, your answers to later questions will lead you back to questions at the appropriate level of difficulty for you
That said, making random guesses can significantly lower your scores. So, if you don't know a correct response, try to eliminate as many answer choices as possible and then select the best one.
For more advice, see the Test-Taking Strategies.

*TIP*
In general, you don't need advanced computer skills to take the GMAT exam. The Data Insights section requires some basic computer skills, such as using tabs to reveal different pages of information, accessing drop-down menus to select answers, and sorting tables. You will also have access to an on-screen calculator in the Data Insights section (calculator is not available in any other section of the exam).
Before test day, it's best to familiarize yourself with the mechanics of taking a computer adaptive test by using the  Official GMAT Practice Exams. They cover such topics as using a mouse, entering an answer, moving on the next question, using the word processor, and accessing the Help function.
Although you'll be able to use the Help function during your actual GMAT exam, the time you spend counts against the time you have to complete the test section.

# The Three GMAT Sections and Question Types: A Quick Overview
Before you get into the details of how to study for specific parts of the GMAT exam, here’s an introduction to the three sections. As you read through, you can click on each section to learn how the particular question types work and smart strategies for answering them.

Five things to know about GMAT exam questions:
1. Only one question or question prompt at a time is presented on the computer screen.
2. The answer choices for the multiple-choice questions will be preceded by radio buttons, rather than by letters.
3. Different question types appear in random order in the multiple-choice and Data Insights sections.
4. You must choose an answer and confirm your choice before moving on to the next question.
5. You can bookmark as many questions as you like, but you can only change your answers to three questions per section. You must finish all your bookmarking, reviewing, and editing within each section’s time limit. No extra time is given to use these new features.

## The Quantitative Reasoning Section
*What the Quantitative section measures:*
Your ability to reason mathematically and solve quantitative problems

*What it consists of:*
21 multiple-choice questions

*Time allowed:*
45 minutes

*The Quantitative section’s type of question is:*
- Problem Solving : Each Problem Solving question consists of a quantitative problem and five answer choices. You select the best answer from the five options given.

## The Verbal Reasoning Section
*What the Verbal section measures:*
Your ability to read and comprehend written material and to reason and evaluate arguments.

*What it consists of:*
23 multiple-choice questions

*Time allowed:*
45 minutes

*The Verbal section’s two types of questions are:*
- Reading Comprehension : Each Reading Comprehension passage comes with several questions that may ask you to interpret material in the passage, draw inferences from it, or apply it to a further context.
The passages are up to about 350 words long. They discuss topics from:
- The social sciences or humanities
- The physical or biological sciences
- Business-related fields, such as marketing, economics, and human resource management
You don’t need specialized knowledge of the subject matter to understand the passages or answer the questions.
- Critical Reasoning : Critical Reasoning questions typically include a short (usually under 100 words) passage that presents a situation, a question based on the passage, and five answer choices. Critical Reasoning questions may also involve a fill-in-the-blank statement and five possible choices to complete the passage.
The materials these questions are based on come from a variety of sources. You don’t need to be familiar with the subject matter to answer correctly.

## The Data Insights Section
*What the Data Insights section measures:*
Your ability to:
- Convert data from multiple sources and in different formats – graphic, numeric, and verbal – into relevant information.
- Organize information to see relationships and solve multiple interrelated problems.
- Combine and manipulate information to solve complex problems that depend on data from one or more sources

*What it consists of:*
20 questions, designed to test your ability to integrate data to solve complex problems. Many questions have multiple parts, and you must answer all parts of a question correctly to receive credit; partial credit will not be given.

*Time allowed:*
45 minutes

*The Data Insights section’s five types of questions are:*
- Multi-Source Reasoning
- Data Sufficiency
- Table Analysis
- Graphics Interpretation
- Two-Part Analysis

# General Test-Taking Strategies 
Studying these broad suggestions will deepen your understanding of the GMAT exam as a whole. When you’re ready for detailed guidance, see Test-taking strategies for specific question types.

1.  *Read the directions carefully in advance.*
    You’ve no doubt heard this advice when taking other tests, but it’s still true: Familiarize yourself ahead of time with the directions for each section of the GMAT exam. They explain exactly what’s required for each type of question.

    Keep in mind: If you need to review directions during the test, click the Help icon. However, the time you take will count against the allotted time for that section.

2.  *Use your allotted testing time wisely.*
    Become familiar in advance with the test, the kinds of questions asked, and directions for each section.

    Keep in mind: Once you start the test, an on-screen clock display will count down the remaining time. You can hide this display, but check the clock periodically to monitor your progress. The clock will automatically alert you by turning yellow and, if hidden, by reappearing when five minutes remain.

3.  *Pace yourself.*
    To make sure you have enough time to answer every question, pay close attention to the number of questions in each section and, again, the amount of time remaining on your on-screen timer. On average, you have just under 2 minutes (117 seconds) for each Verbal question, about 2 minutes, 9 seconds for each Quantitative question, and about 2 minutes, 15 seconds for each Data Insights question. Evaluate your time halfway through the test to decide if you should speed up or can afford to spend a little more time on each question.

    Keep in mind: Try not to spend so much time on one question that you neglect others. Make an educated guess if you don’t know an answer or if a question is too time-consuming. You don’t want to be forced to guess randomly at the end because you’ve run out of time. Remember, you can review and edit your answers at the end of each section. You can only change your answers to three questions per section and you must finish all your reviewing and editing within each section’s time limit.

4.  *Read the questions and the answer choices carefully and thoroughly.*
    Before answering a question, determine exactly what’s being asked. Avoid skimming a question or the possible answers; you may miss important information or nuances.

    Keep in mind: If you don’t know the correct answer, or if the question is too time-consuming, try to eliminate choices you know are wrong, select the best of the remaining choices, and move on. Keep moving through the test and try to finish each section.

5.  *Confirm each answer only when you’re certain you want to move on to the next question.*
    Remember that you cannot change your answers once you move to the next question. This is especially important in the Data Insights, Quantitative, and Verbal sections of the GMAT exam, which use computer-adaptive testing and select the next question you see based upon your previous responses. You can review and edit your answers at the end of each section but can only change your answers to three questions per section. 

6.  *Again, strive to complete each section.*
    If you don’t finish in the allotted time, you’ll still receive scores as long as you’ve worked on every GMAT section. But because the score reflects the number of questions answered, your score will decrease with each unanswered question. This can translate to a severe penalty for failing to finish. So, do your best to answer each question!
